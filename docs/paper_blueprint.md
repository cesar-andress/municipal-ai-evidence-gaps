## Complete paper structure

### Abstract

#### Purpose
Summarise the problem, method, core descriptive findings (once available), and contributions in ≤250 words for GIQ submission format.

#### Expected content
- Context: municipalities publish AI strategies, registers, and transparency pages
- Gap: transparency of commitments may not supply programme-level governance evidence
- Method: comparative observability analysis across D1–D5 dimensions
- Findings placeholder: descriptive patterns of observable vs. under-observable evidence (to be filled post-coding)
- Implications: accountability limitations for external reviewers relying on public documents
- Explicit canonical non-claims: no readiness scoring; no municipal ranking; no legal compliance determination; no LocalGovBench validation; no inference of internal governance quality

#### Expected evidence
- Corpus size and document-type distribution (post-collection)
- One headline observability pattern per RQ (post-coding)
- Reference to open protocol and artifact

#### Claims allowed
- Public documentation exhibits patterned evidence gaps
- Observability varies across dimensions and document types
- Findings have implications for government information policy and accountability

#### Claims prohibited
- Municipalities "lack governance" or "fail" at AI governance
- Legal non-compliance or AI Act breach
- Municipal ranking or performance league tables
- Causal claims (e.g., transparency causes accountability failure)
- Validation or refutation of LocalGovBench

### 1. Introduction

#### Purpose
Establish stakes, define the puzzle, state RQs and contributions, and orient the reader to what the paper does and does not claim.

#### Expected content
- Opening: municipal AI adoption and proliferation of public governance documentation
- Puzzle: visibility of principles vs. thin programme-level evidence
- Working proposition to investigate (not assert pre-empirically)
- Three research questions (verbatim):
  - **RQ1.** What governance evidence is publicly observable in municipal AI documentation?
  - **RQ2.** Which governance dimensions are systematically under-observable?
  - **RQ3.** What patterns of transparency and evidence gaps emerge across municipalities and document types?
- Three contributions (commitment-heavy/artefact-thin profile; genre inversion; open coding materials)
- Roadmap paragraph
- Boundary statement: observability analysis, public documents only

#### Expected evidence
- 2–3 illustrative (anonymised or general) examples of commitment-heavy public pages—drawn from literature or pilot notes, not fabricated statistics
- Citation of transparency and algorithmic governance scholarship
- No empirical counts until Findings section

#### Claims allowed
- There is a policy and scholarly need to examine what public documents make observable
- Programme-level assessment from public documents may be constrained by evidence gaps
- The study addresses a timely GIQ-relevant information problem

#### Claims prohibited
- Pre-emptive empirical conclusions ("most cities lack oversight evidence")
- Normative condemnation of municipalities
- Implying internal practices are weak because public docs are thin
- LocalGovBench validation or legal compliance determination framing

### 2. Related Work

#### Purpose
Position the study within government information, algorithmic transparency, municipal digital government, and accountability scholarship; identify the gap this manuscript fills.

#### Expected content
- **Government information and transparency** — open government, proactive disclosure, information quality
- **Algorithmic and AI transparency** — registers, impact assessments, transparency tools
- **Municipal digital government** — local AI strategies, capacity, multi-level governance
- **Accountability and documentary governance** — evidence chains, answerability, oversight
- **Ceremonial compliance and administrative burden** — symbolic adoption vs. substantive documentation
- **Gap statement** — prior work documents transparency adoption but less often maps programme-level evidence availability in municipal AI documentation comparatively

#### Expected evidence
- 25–40 peer-reviewed sources (target range for GIQ)
- Synthesis table optional: streams × what they measure × what this study adds

#### Claims allowed
- Literature tends to focus on presence of transparency instruments or high-level principles
- Observability of programme-level evidence is under-theorised and under-measured
- This study extends (not replaces) existing transparency research

#### Claims prohibited
- Dismissing prior literature as "wrong"
- Claiming no prior work exists on algorithmic transparency
- Positioning the paper as a LocalGovBench validation study

- Five dimensions D1–D5 with programme-level rationale
- Conceptual model (figure from [`conceptual_model.md`](conceptual_model.md))
- Relationships to transparency, accountability, ceremonial compliance, administrative burden
- Distinction: Public-Document Observability ≠ inference of internal governance quality ≠ readiness scoring ≠ legal compliance determination
- Propositions P1–P4 as analytic expectations (not findings)

#### Expected evidence
- Conceptual figure (Mermaid/exported for manuscript)
- Definitions grounded in cited theory (Bovens; Meijer & Curtin; Moynihan et al.)
- Coding label definitions aligned with [`coding_protocol.md`](coding_protocol.md)

#### Claims allowed
- Public-Document Observability is an operational lens for uniform documentary coding
- Evidence gaps create accountability limitations **for external reviewers using public documents**
- Commitment-heavy, evidence-light documentation is a plausible pattern to investigate

#### Claims prohibited
- Treating the conceptual model as confirmed before empirics
- Reifying observability as a performance score
- Claiming gaps prove ceremonial compliance in specific municipalities (pre-empirical)

## Reviewer attack map

### Attack 1: "Transparency is not governance"

| Element | Response |
|---------|----------|
| **Why it may occur** | Reviewers fear the paper equates publication with governance quality or implies municipalities with thin docs govern poorly |
| **How to respond** | State in Introduction, Conceptual Framework, and Limitations that **observability ≠ governance quality**. The paper studies what the public record makes available to external reviewers, not internal practice. Use "accountability limitations for external assessment" language consistently. Cite transparency-insufficiency literature. Never use "governance failure" phrasing. |

### Attack 2: "Documentation is not practice"

| Element | Response |
|---------|----------|
| **Why it may occur** | Public administration reviewers distinguish formal documents from street-level / organisational practice |
| **How to respond** | Acknowledge directly: the study **does not observe practice**. Contribution is precisely to bound what practice-external assessors can know from public documents. Frame as **epistemic boundary analysis** for democratic oversight, auditors, and researchers. Position internal practice as explicitly out of scope and future work requiring different methods (interviews, FOI, ethnography). |

### Attack 3: "Small sample / not representative"

| Element | Response |
|---------|----------|
| **Why it may occur** | GIQ reviewers expect clarity on generalisability; European municipal AI docs are heterogeneous |
| **How to respond** | Describe sampling as **purposive comparative** (not representative of all EU municipalities). Power the study on **cross-case pattern identification** across dimensions and document types, not population inference. Report sample transparently in Method and Limitations. Avoid overgeneralising language in Discussion. Optional: plan v0.2 expansion as future work. |

### Attack 4: "Public documents only — major selection bias"

| Element | Response |
|---------|----------|
| **Why it may occur** | Reviewers note that important governance evidence may exist only in internal records |
| **How to respond** | Treat public-document boundary as **constitutive of the research question**, not a bug: many oversight actors (citizens, journalists, competing councillors, researchers) only have public access. The paper asks what accountability is possible through that channel. Discuss internal–public gap as limitation and future research. Reference ethics advantages and reproducibility benefits. |

### Attack 5: "No causal claims / merely descriptive"

| Element | Response |
|---------|----------|
| **Why it may occur** | Reviewers want mechanism or explanatory theory |
| **How to respond** | Embrace descriptive comparative design as appropriate for **early-stage mapping** of a new documentation field. Use theory to interpret patterns (burden, ceremonial compliance) as **plausible explanations**, not tested causes. State explicitly: no causal identification in v0.1. Contribution is conceptual + empirical mapping + protocol. |

### Attack 6: "Why not score readiness or compliance?"

| Element | Response |
|---------|----------|
| **Why it may occur** | AI governance field is saturated with indices, audits, and benchmark proposals |
| **How to respond** | Explain that readiness scoring would **over-interpret** public silence and violate epistemic bounds. Readiness scoring implies inference of internal governance quality. Instead, Public-Document Observability analysis supports **informed** use of benchmarks by showing what observable evidence public documentation can supply. Cite [`non_claims.md`](non_claims.md) and [`global_editorial_style_guide.md`](global_editorial_style_guide.md) in Method footnote or box. |

### Attack 7: "Coding subjectivity / inter-coder reliability"

| Element | Response |
|---------|----------|
| **Why it may occur** | Qualitative coding of governance evidence invites consistency challenges |
| **How to respond** | Report double-coding on ≥10–20% subset; publish coding protocol and decision rules in artifact; document disagreements and rule refinements; use structured labels (not Likert readiness scales). Pre-register dimension definitions in protocol. |

### Attack 8: "European focus limits contribution"

| Element | Response |
|---------|----------|
| **Why it may occur** | GIQ is international; reviewers ask about transferability |
| **How to respond** | Justify Europe: advanced municipal AI documentation activity, AI Act context (without compliance claims), multi-country comparison. Discuss transferability as structural (documentation logic) not jurisdictional scores. Offer protocol for reuse in other regions. |
