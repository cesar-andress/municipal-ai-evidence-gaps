## 4. Primary recommendation

**Primary target: Government Information Quarterly (GIQ)**

### Rationale

1. **Object of study.** The manuscript's dependent variable is *government information*—what municipal AI governance evidence is publicly documented—not internal programme performance. GIQ is the most direct scholarly venue for this question.

2. **Transparency and information policy.** GIQ readers expect analysis of how governments publish, structure, and maintain information for accountability. The transparency-vs-evidence distinction maps cleanly onto government information policy debates.

3. **Municipal digital government.** GIQ publishes extensively on local e-government, open data, and public-sector algorithmic governance. A European comparative documentary study extends this line with a programme-level evidence lens.

4. **Methods and reproducibility.** The open coding protocol, schema, and corpus infrastructure align with emerging expectations for transparent empirical research without turning the paper into a software article.

5. **Title and framing.** *When Transparency Is Not Enough* is an information-quality claim, not a management performance claim—strengthening GIQ fit over PMR or TGPPP.

### Secondary options

- **Information Polity** — if the manuscript emphasises democratic accountability and institutional theory over information infrastructure
- **PMR** — if reframed around administrative burden and ceremonial compliance with reduced protocol detail
- **TGPPP** — if reframed as municipal digital transformation practice with case-narrative depth

## 5. Positioning statement for cover letter (draft)

> This manuscript contributes to Government Information Quarterly's scholarship on public-sector information and algorithmic governance by reporting a commitment-heavy, artefact-thin evidence-gap profile in municipal AI public documentation, documenting genre inversion between purpose-built transparency instruments and normative strategies or frameworks, and releasing open coding materials for bounded replication. Public-Document Observability is applied as an operational lens, not as a standalone theoretical contribution. The study advances government information research without scoring municipal readiness, ranking cities, or claiming legal compliance.
