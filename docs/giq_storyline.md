**Target journal:** Government Information Quarterly  
**Title:** When Transparency Is Not Enough: Evidence Gaps in Municipal AI Governance  
**Subtitle:** A Comparative Analysis of European Municipal AI Documentation

This document defines the **narrative arc** the manuscript must sustain from abstract through conclusion. It is a storyline guide, not a draft manuscript.

## Storyline in one paragraph

European municipalities are publishing AI governance documents at accelerating speed, signalling transparency and accountability to citizens and overseers. Yet government information scholarship has long cautioned that disclosure can outrun the evidentiary substance oversight requires. This manuscript asks a GIQ-centred question: **what programme-level governance evidence do public municipal AI documents actually make observable—and where do systematic evidence gaps constrain independent assessment?** By introducing public-document observability and mapping gaps across five programme-level dimensions, the study shows when transparency is necessary but insufficient, and what this means for accountability through the public record.

## Act I — Problem

### Narrative beat
Municipal AI is moving from pilot projects to programme-level portfolios. Local governments face pressure to **publish** strategies, registers, and transparency pages—both to meet expectations and to demonstrate responsible innovation.

### What the reader should feel
The problem is not whether municipalities mention AI ethics. The problem is whether **government information** about municipal AI programmes is structured so external actors can assess how programmes are actually governed.

### GIQ hook
Government information systems are being asked to support algorithmic accountability. We do not yet know enough about **what programme-level evidence** those information systems expose in practice.

### Drafting cue
Open with the information-policy stakes, not a technology hype frame.

---

## Act II — Transparency claims

### Narrative beat
Municipalities and policy discourse increasingly **claim transparency** through visible artefacts: AI strategies, algorithm registers, responsible-AI statements, open-government portals.

### Scholarly tension
Transparency is treated as a precondition for accountability. But transparency research warns of **symbolic disclosure**—documents that make commitments visible without enabling meaningful scrutiny.

### What to establish
- Transparency initiatives are widespread and politically salient
- Presence of a document ≠ presence of programme-level evidence
- GIQ readers should recognise their own literature (open government, proactive disclosure, information quality)

### Drafting cue
Name the risk: **transparency claims can outpace documentary evidence**.

---

## Act III — Missing evidence

### Narrative beat
Programme-level governance assessment requires observable artefacts: named ownership, human oversight routines, log and data governance, vendor stewardship, incident and lifecycle accountability.

In many domains, public municipal AI documentation appears **rich in principles** and **thin in programme-level artefacts**—but this is an empirical question the manuscript must test, not assume in the storyline.

### The pivot sentence (template)
> The question is not whether municipalities publish AI documents, but whether those documents supply the evidence an independent reviewer needs to assess programme-level governance.

### What to avoid
Do not prejudge which dimensions are thinnest before coding. The storyline sets up **expectation to investigate**, not a foregone conclusion.

### Drafting cue
Introduce D1–D5 as the programme-level evidence domains the public record should make observable if assessment is to be possible.

---

## Act IV — Evidence gap concept

### Narrative beat
The manuscript applies **public-document observability** as an **operational lens** and maps **evidence gaps** as empirical findings.

- **PDO (operational lens):** uniform coding rules for what can be identified and quoted from public official documents
- **Evidence gap:** dimensions where evidence is partial, indirect, or not publicly observable

### Conceptual move
Shift the conversation from "is there a strategy/register?" to "what governance evidence does the public record structurally provide or withhold?"

### Boundary move (critical for GIQ)
Evidence gaps are **accountability limitations** for external reviewers, not proof of internal failure, legal non-compliance, or readiness scoring.

### Drafting cue
Deploy the conceptual model figure here. This act is the paper's **theoretical product**.

---

## Act V — Comparative analysis

### Narrative beat
The study conducts a **comparative documentary analysis** of European municipal AI public documentation, coding observability across municipalities, document types, and dimensions D1–D5.

### Method as story
Researchers become external reviewers limited to the public record—mirroring the epistemic position of citizens, councillors, journalists, and scholars without administrative access.

### Empirical promise (post-collection)

**RQ1.** What governance evidence is publicly observable in municipal AI documentation?

**RQ2.** Which governance dimensions are systematically under-observable?

**RQ3.** What patterns of transparency and evidence gaps emerge across municipalities and document types?

### What to avoid
Municipal ranking. Readiness scoring. "Best" and "worst" cities. Legal compliance determination matrices.

### Drafting cue
Present findings as **maps of observability**, not scorecards.

---

## Act VI — Implications for accountability

### Narrative beat
If public documentation emphasises commitments over programme-level artefacts, **accountability through the public record** is structurally constrained—even when municipalities maintain internal governance routines not visible externally.

**Discussion implication (not an empirical RQ):** What are the implications of these evidence gaps for independent governance assessment based on public documentation alone?

### Implications ladder

| Audience | Implication |
|----------|-------------|
| **Government information professionals** | Structure registers and transparency pages to surface programme-level artefacts, not only principles |
| **Municipal policymakers** | Publication strategies should address ownership, oversight, logs, vendor stewardship, lifecycle accountability |
| **Oversight bodies & researchers** | Public-document-based assessment has epistemic limits; methods must match available evidence |
| **GIQ scholarly community** | Observability analysis should complement transparency adoption studies |

### Closing tone
Measured, non-punitive, forward-looking. The goal is stronger **government information** for municipal AI accountability—not naming and shaming cities.

### Final line (template)
> Transparency remains necessary for democratic oversight of municipal AI, but government information policy must also ask whether published documents supply the programme-level evidence accountability actually requires.

## Elevator pitch (30 seconds)

Municipalities publish AI strategies and registers to be transparent—but what governance evidence do those documents actually reveal? We report a commitment-heavy, artefact-thin evidence-gap profile, genre inversion across document types, and robustness checks in a comparative European municipal corpus. The paper advances GIQ's government information agenda without municipal ranking, readiness scoring, or legal compliance determination.

## Cover letter storyline (draft)

Dear Editor,

We submit a manuscript for *Government Information Quarterly* examining what programme-level governance evidence European municipal AI documentation makes publicly observable. As municipalities publish strategies, registers, and transparency pages, policymakers and researchers increasingly rely on public documents to assess AI governance. Yet government information scholarship suggests that transparency can foreground commitments while leaving programme-level evidence thin.

Our study applies **Public-Document Observability** as an operational lens and reports three empirical patterns in European municipal AI documentation: a commitment-heavy, artefact-thin profile (primary), genre inversion between purpose-built transparency instruments and normative strategies or frameworks (secondary), and open coding materials for bounded replication (supporting). Retrieved-only sensitivity analysis and inter-rater reliability (κ = 0.81) support the principal patterns—maintaining the canonical non-claims: no readiness scoring; no municipal ranking; no legal compliance determination; no LocalGovBench validation; no inference of internal governance quality.

We believe the manuscript fits GIQ's scholarship on government information, algorithmic transparency, and public-sector accountability.

Sincerely,  
[Authors]
